## Methodology
Population baseline: NAICS-2 utilities sector (NAICS 22, encompassing electric, gas, water, sewage, waste), assuming NO CI overlay applied (per spec §6.4 stacking discipline).

Frequency lift derivation:
Water utilities within NAICS-22 carry an asymmetric *attack-surface* signal: the sector population is large (~152k drinking-water systems per CISA) and predominantly small/under-resourced municipal operators with documented baseline-control gaps (EPA's 2023 inspection finding that ~70% of systems lacked full cybersecurity baseline implementation). Documented nation-state interest is real and recent: the Aliquippa November 2023 incident was the entry point of a multi-utility CyberAv3ngers campaign that compromised at least 7 US water utilities within weeks via Internet-exposed Unitronics Vision-series PLCs. Oldsmar 2021 — although attribution is disputed — remains the canonical small-water-utility OT-exposure case study. CISA Water/Wastewater advisory volume rose materially across 2023 and 2024. The frequency lift over the NAICS-22 aggregate is moderate, not extreme: water utilities are *more* targeted than the aggregate (justifying lift above 1×) but the dominant attack pattern (opportunistic Internet-exposed PLC compromise) yields lower per-incident impact than nation-state-coordinated grid attacks. Locked frequency lift: `1.7×`, mid-range within the §10.4 plausible 1.5–2.0× window.

Magnitude lift derivation:
Water-utility magnitude tail is dominated by *safety-critical* loss components (potential public-health harm from chemical-setpoint manipulation) that no realized incident has yet materialized into publicly disclosed dollar figures. Anchors: (1) Oldsmar 2021 — chlorine setpoint 100→11,100 ppm; if undetected, downstream public-health exposure could have produced mass-casualty regulatory/litigation tail in the hundreds of millions; (2) Aliquippa 2023 — booster-station-only impact; service disruption to a single municipality but no public-health harm; (3) the EPA's regulatory regime (Safe Drinking Water Act civil penalties, state-PUC enforcement) creates a non-trivial regulatory-tail component on any safety-relevant incident. NetDiligence 2024 public-sector/utilities supplement shows business-interruption fraction elevated for small-utility claims relative to all-industry. The IRIS NAICS-22 aggregate magnitude is pulled higher by electric utilities (industrial-scale outage costs); water utilities sit *below* the NAICS-22 aggregate on realized BI dollars but sit *above* on tail-risk severity (public-health potentiality). On net, the magnitude lift is modest — anchored to realized BI patterns plus a regulatory-tail adjustment, with the safety-critical tail acknowledged in limitations. Locked magnitude lift: `1.8×`, FLOOR of the §10.4 plausible 1.8–2.5× window. Floor positioning is intentional: zero realized US water-utility cyber-physical loss disclosures exist; safety-critical tail (Oldsmar near-miss) is real but pricing it into an auto-applied multiplier without realized-loss anchors would be precautionary pricing, not empirical fidelity. Multiplier moves to mid-range upon a realized US water-utility incident with publicly disclosed audited financial loss.

## Known limitations
- Empirical gap: no realized water-utility cyber-physical incident has produced a publicly disclosed audited financial-loss figure of comparable specificity to Colonial Pipeline's $4.4M ransom or Norsk Hydro's $70M loss disclosure. The magnitude lift relies on regulatory-tail extrapolation, public-health-tail potentiality, and NetDiligence claim-study public-sector/utility patterns rather than a single anchor incident.
- Oldsmar 2021 attribution is disputed and the originally reported attacker-via-TeamViewer narrative has been partially walked back by the Pinellas County Sheriff's Office; the incident remains canonically referenced but its forensic certainty is lower than originally portrayed. Treated as an attack-class anchor, not a forensic anchor.
- Water-sector population is heavily skewed toward small municipal operators; the multiplier reflects a sector-aggregate posture and may *understate* lift for the largest 100 metropolitan water utilities (which face concentrated nation-state interest) and *overstate* lift for the smallest rural operators (where cyber-relevant attack surface may be nil).
- Magnitude multiplier locked at floor of plausible range (1.8×, not mid-range 2.0×) pending a realized US water-utility incident with audited financials. The safety-critical public-health tail (per Oldsmar 2021 setpoint manipulation analysis) is acknowledged but not priced into the multiplier — pricing an unrealized catastrophic potential without empirical loss data would be precautionary pricing dressed as calibration.
- Stacking concern: the existing CI overlay (`critical_infrastructure`, freq 1.4× mag 2.0×) was calibrated against a population that included CI-designated water utilities. Multiplicative composition (combined freq 2.38×, combined mag 4.0× over NAICS-2 baseline) double-counts a small portion. Mitigation: stacking-pin test + spec §11.1 recalibration commitment.
- 2-axis multiplier conflates OT vulnerability into frequency lift; spec §11.2 forward-compat for third axis.
