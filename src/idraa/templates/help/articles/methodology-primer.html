The FAIR model — concept spine

Idraa is built on FAIR (Factor Analysis of Information Risk), an Open FAIR™ standard for expressing cyber risk as a financial quantity. Every number the engine produces traces back to the three relationships below. Understanding them tells you exactly what you are estimating when you fill in a scenario form.

1. Risk = Loss Event Frequency × Loss Magnitude

How often does a harmful event occur, and how much does it cost each time? Those two quantities — Open FAIR nodes Loss Event Frequency (LEF) and Loss Magnitude (LM) — are the top-level factors. Risk is their product, sampled thousands of times across your input ranges by Monte Carlo simulation.

2. LEF = Threat Event Frequency × Vulnerability

Not every attempt causes a loss. Threat Event Frequency (TEF) counts how often a threat actor tries the attack per year. Vulnerability is the probability that any one attempt succeeds. Loss Event Frequency is their product: the expected number of successful harmful events per year.

3. Vulnerability = P(Threat Capability > Control Strength)

Formally, Vulnerability is the probability that a threat actor’s capability exceeds the asset’s resistance. In the scenario wizard you author the inherent Vulnerability — the baseline before any of your controls are applied. The controls you tick are modelled separately and reduce this probability in the simulation; entering a mentally-discounted figure would credit them twice. The Open FAIR nodes involved are Threat Capability (TC) and Control Strength / Resistance Strength (CS).

4. LM = Primary Loss + Secondary Loss (per loss event)

Loss Magnitude is the total cost per loss event, not an annual figure. It splits into two Open FAIR nodes: Primary Loss (PL) — direct costs such as incident response, recovery, and ransom — and Secondary Loss (SL) — knock-on costs such as regulatory fines, lost business, and reputational damage. The engine annualises them by multiplying each simulated per-event magnitude by the simulated event count for that year.

ALE: the mean annual loss

Annualized Loss Expectancy (ALE) is the mean of the simulated annual-loss distribution — the long-run expected loss per year. It is a v3 view-model derivation (not itself a named Open FAIR node) computed from the LEF × LM product distribution. Because cyber-loss distributions are heavily right-skewed, the mean exceeds the median; ALE always refers to the mean, never the median. Use the Run & read analyses article for guidance on reading the full distribution rather than relying on ALE alone.

Monte Carlo simulation

Nobody knows the exact value of TEF, Vulnerability, PL, or SL. You enter a low–high range — the bounds you are 90% confident contain the true value (formally, the 5th and 95th percentiles of your belief distribution). The engine draws thousands of random samples from that range, computes Risk = LEF × LM for each draw, and collects the results into an annual-loss distribution. The result is not one number but a realistic spread: the typical year, a bad year, and the rare disaster.

The 5th and 95th percentile framing is a 90% credible interval, not hard min/max limits. For frequency and magnitude inputs the sampled distribution has tails that extend beyond your stated range — this is intentional, because rare extremes are exactly what risk analysis must not truncate away. Vulnerability is always a probability (0–100%) and is always sampled within the range you enter.

Lognormal authoring — the distribution assumption

Cyber loss quantities (frequency, magnitude) are modelled as lognormal distributions: non-negative, right-skewed, with a heavy upper tail. The lognormal assumption is well-supported in the calibration literature. Douglas Hubbard and Richard Seiersen note (in How to Measure Anything in Cybersecurity Risk, 2nd ed., 2023, ch. 6) that 90% credible intervals elicited as low–high estimates map naturally onto lognormal parameters via the 5th–95th percentile pair. Jack Jones and the FAIR Institute document the same distributional assumption in the FAIR-CAM calibration framework.

When you enter a low and a high estimate, Idraa derives the lognormal mean and sigma that place those values at the 5th and 95th percentiles respectively. The sampled distribution is untruncated — the tails extend beyond your stated low and high, reflecting the reality that rare events can exceed expert expectations. This is why a “high” estimate is not a ceiling; it is where you are 95% confident the true value falls below.
