What a scenario is

A scenario is one specific way your organisation could suffer a loss — for example, ransomware encrypting your production servers, or an insider copying customer data. Each scenario is modelled independently; you can include multiple scenarios in a single analysis run to see your combined exposure.

The scenario wizard collects four FAIR inputs. Each is entered as a low–high range — the range you are 90% confident contains the true value (formally, the 5th and 95th percentiles). The engine samples across that range thousands of times (Monte Carlo simulation) to produce a realistic spread of outcomes rather than a single point. For the statistical foundations, see the FAIR methodology primer.

The four FAIR inputs

1. Threat Event Frequency (TEF)

How often the threat actor tries the attack against this asset, expressed as a number of attempts per year. Enter the low and high ends of your 90% range. A ransomware group scanning your public-facing systems might attempt entry dozens of times a year; a targeted campaign against a specific OT network might be much rarer. The industry-calibrated starting point displayed in the wizard is an IRIS-2025 industry median baseline — a starting prior to be adjusted for your organisation’s context, not a definitive value.

2. Vulnerability

The probability that an attempt succeeds — that is, the chance the threat actor’s capability exceeds the asset’s resistance. In FAIR terms: P(Threat Capability > Control Strength).

Critical framing: you are estimating the asset’s inherent susceptibility, before counting your controls. Do not mentally discount the figure for protections you already have — the controls you tick are modelled separately and reduce it in the analysis. Counting them here would credit them twice. The industry pre-fill is labelled “inherent baseline” for the same reason.

Vulnerability is always a probability (0–100%). The low and high inputs bound the 90% range and the engine samples within it; it never exceeds 100%. The IRIS-2025 industry median shown as a starting point is a median, not a prescription — your SME judgement adjusts it up or down.

3. Primary Loss

The direct financial cost per loss event — what you spend when a single incident occurs: incident response, system recovery, ransom payment, productivity loss during downtime. This is a per-event magnitude, not an annual figure. The engine multiplies it by Loss Event Frequency across thousands of simulated years to produce an annualised loss distribution. Enter low and high estimates in dollars for that 90% range.

4. Secondary Loss

The knock-on cost per loss event — fines, regulatory penalties, reputational damage, lost customers, and legal liability that arise after the incident. Like Primary Loss, this is a per-event magnitude. The two are added to form Loss Magnitude for each simulated event; annualisation happens in the Monte Carlo layer, not in your input. If secondary consequences are negligible or unknown, set both low and high to zero and revisit later.

SME estimates and pooling

For each input, the wizard lets you record estimates from several subject-matter experts. When you have multiple expert judgements, Idraa pools them into one distribution using percentile pooling — pooled ranges preserve more of the between-expert uncertainty than a simple collapse would.

The industry calibration baselines shown in the wizard are IRIS-2025 industry median values — the midpoint of observed industry distributions, not population means. They are starting priors designed to be SME-adjusted for your organisation’s specific context, threat landscape, and asset exposure.

Controls step

After the four FAIR inputs, the wizard asks you to tick the controls that apply to this scenario. Controls are modelled as effectiveness reductions on Threat Event Frequency and Vulnerability in the simulation; they do not alter the inherent Vulnerability estimate you entered. This is why you author inherent Vulnerability independently and let the engine apply controls on top. Adopt controls from the Control Library or create custom ones at Controls.

Worked example — mid-sized manufacturer

Suppose you run security for a mid-sized manufacturer with roughly 1,500 staff and $400 million in annual revenue. Here is the shortest path to a useful answer. (Numbers below are illustrative to show input shape — not recommended values for your business.)

  1. Organisation setup: industry “Manufacturing”, size “Large”, revenue $400M, maturity “Developing”. This sets the IRIS-2025 industry median baselines in the wizard pre-fills.
  2. Controls: adopt three from the library — multi-factor sign-in, immutable (tamper-proof) backups, and email filtering — and enter each one’s yearly cost.
  3. Scenario — “Ransomware on production servers”:
    • Threat Event Frequency (TEF): low 1, high 4 attempts per year. (A mid-market manufacturing firm is a plausible ransomware target; the range reflects uncertainty in campaign cadence.)
    • Vulnerability: low 10%, high 30% — the inherent chance an attempt succeeds, before counting your controls. The three controls above will be applied in the simulation to reduce this.
    • Primary Loss per loss event: low $250,000, high $2,000,000 (recovery labour, downtime, potential ransom).
    • Secondary Loss per loss event: low $100,000, high $1,500,000 (regulatory fines, customer churn, reputational damage).
    Tick the three controls above as reducing this scenario, then save.
  4. Run it. Navigate to Analyses → New, select this scenario, and run. Idraa returns a distribution of annual losses, the expected (mean) annual loss (ALE), and the risk-reduction value and ROI for each control. See Run & read analyses for a guide to reading the results page.

That single scenario is enough to start a budget conversation. Add more scenarios over time — phishing, OT network intrusion, supply-chain compromise — to build a fuller picture of your exposure.

Starting from the Scenario Library

Rather than filling every field from scratch, browse the Scenario Library for a ready-made entry close to your threat, adopt it into your workspace, and adjust the low–high ranges for your context. Library entries carry IRIS-2025 industry median calibration baselines; your SME adjustments layer on top via the wizard. When you adopt a scenario from a different industry, the Primary Loss and Secondary Loss pre-fills are re-scaled to your organisation’s revenue tier (the entry’s industry tag is advisory and is not used in the rescale); the Impact-page calibration banner shows the multiplier applied — review and tweak every value before saving. Admins can also bulk-import scenarios from CSV or JSON at Scenarios → Import.
