Running a Monte Carlo analysis

Once you have built at least one scenario and ticked its controls, navigate to Analyses → New. Select one or more scenarios to include in the run and click Run analysis.

Idraa runs a Monte Carlo simulation: it samples across your low–high input ranges thousands of times, each time computing an annual loss figure, to produce a realistic spread of outcomes — the typical year, a bad year, and the rare but costly disaster. The default is 10,000 iterations; the server enforces a maximum of 100,000 iterations. More iterations improve tail precision but take longer.

For the statistical foundations behind the simulation, see the FAIR methodology primer.

Single scenario vs. aggregate run

A single-scenario run simulates one threat scenario in isolation and is the right starting point when assessing a specific risk or justifying a targeted control investment.

An aggregate run includes several scenarios and reports their combined annual loss exposure — the sum of per-scenario annualised loss distributions plus the associated control attribution across all included scenarios. Use it to size total cyber-insurance limits or present an organisation-wide risk posture to a board or risk committee. Aggregate outputs are additive summaries of independent FAIR scenarios; they do not apply portfolio-finance adjustments such as correlation weighting.

Reading the outputs

Each result is built to drive a specific decision. Here is who each output is for and what to do with it.

OutputWho it’s forWhat to do with it
Annualized Loss Expectancy (ALE) & Residual ALE Board / CFO Your expected (mean) annual loss in dollars, before and after controls. ALE is the mean of the simulated annual-loss distribution — because cyber-loss distributions are right-skewed, the mean exceeds the median, and ALE is the correct summary for budgeting purposes. Compare Residual ALE against your organisation’s stated risk appetite.
Return on Investment (ROI) & Control Value Security leader / budget owner Decide which controls are worth funding: how much annual loss each control removes relative to its yearly cost.
Loss Exceedance Curve & Value at Risk (VaR) Risk committee / insurance Understand the bad-year, worst-case tail. The Loss Exceedance Curve plots P(annual loss ≥ X) for each dollar amount X. Value at Risk (VaR) at a chosen confidence level q is the q-th percentile of the simulated annual-loss distribution — the threshold you are q% confident you will not exceed in a given year. Use VaR levels to size cyber-insurance limits or set board-level risk tolerances.
Control matrix Security team See which controls help which scenarios — a to-do list for what to implement or strengthen next.

Expected Shortfall (ES)

The results page also shows Expected Shortfall (ES) at selected confidence levels (95%, 99%, 99.9%). ES at level q is the mean of all simulated annual-loss outcomes that fall at or above the q-th percentile — the average of the worst (1 − q)% of years. ES is a conditional tail mean, not a single worst-case figure: it answers “given that we end up in the tail, how bad is the average outcome?”

ES is more informative than VaR for tail risk because it captures the severity of losses beyond the threshold, not just their probability.

Tail reliability at default iteration counts

At the default 10,000 iterations, the deepest tail — around the 99.9th percentile (VaR 99.9% and ES 99.9%) — rests on approximately 10 samples. This makes those figures the least reliable readings on the page. Treat them as indicative. If tail precision matters (for example, when sizing insurance limits at the 99.9% level), increase the iteration count toward the 100,000 server maximum and re-run. Mid-range confidence levels (VaR 95% and ES 99%) are substantially more reliable at the default count.

Random seed and reproducibility

Every run uses a fixed random seed (default 42) so that identical inputs plus the same seed always produce identical numbers — making results reproducible across re-runs and between colleagues. The seed is shown on the run results page and is included in the exported PDF.

Because a fixed seed locks in one particular Monte Carlo realisation, it also locks in a specific tail shape. To see sampling variability — especially in the deep tail (e.g. p99.9, which rests on ≈ 10 samples at 10,000 iterations) — vary the seed across several runs and compare results, or raise the iteration count toward the 100,000 server maximum. The seed is a simulation parameter only; it is not a security or cryptographic value.

Downloading the data

Every chart on the results page has a Download data (CSV) button in its toolbar. Use it to pull the underlying simulation numbers into a spreadsheet or your own reporting pipeline. For a formatted executive summary, see the Reports guide.
